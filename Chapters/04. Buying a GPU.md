# Buying a GPU: From Single Card to Multi-GPU AI Server

## Chapter Introduction

In the previous chapters, we built a complete mental model for local AI hardware.

We learned how language models store their parameters, how quantization shrinks them, and how to calculate whether a model will actually fit inside a given amount of VRAM.

Then we learned that fitting is not the same as running well — that **memory bandwidth** decides how fast a model can generate tokens once it is loaded, and that a smaller, faster machine can outrun a larger, slower one.

Both of those chapters answered "can it run, and how fast."

This chapter answers a different question, one that eventually comes up for almost everyone who gets serious about local AI:

> **"Okay. I understand all of this now. So what do I actually buy?"**

This sounds like it should be the easiest question in the whole handbook.

It is not.

Walk into any local AI forum or Discord server and ask "what GPU should I buy?" and you will get a dozen confident, contradictory answers within minutes. Someone will tell you to buy a Mac. Someone else will tell you Macs are useless for AI. Someone will tell you to buy four RTX 4090s. Someone will tell you four RTX 4090s in one machine is a fire hazard waiting to happen.

Most of this confusion exists for the same reason the previous chapter existed: people are comparing the wrong numbers, or comparing the right numbers without understanding what stands between the GPU and the rest of the computer.

A GPU does not work alone. It sits inside a motherboard, talks to a CPU through a physical interface, shares space with other expansion cards, and eventually has to cooperate with other GPUs if you ever want to scale up. Every one of those relationships has its own bottleneck, and every one of those bottlenecks is invisible on a spec sheet unless you know what to look for.

This chapter is the buying guide that ties everything together. We will cover:

* How to think about your budget as a *category*, not just a number.
* What actually determines whether a GPU is "good" for local AI.
* PCIe — the physical pathway that connects every GPU to everything else, explained completely from zero.
* Why some motherboards quietly strangle your second GPU before you even notice.
* Two real hardware case studies: a consumer dual-GPU desktop, and a four-GPU Threadripper Pro workstation.
* Why gaming hardware is the wrong foundation for an AI box.
* A tier list of GPUs, from the best available today down to sensible budget picks.
* How a build changes as you scale from 1 GPU to 16 GPUs.
* A short list of tools and terms you'll keep running into while you shop and build.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to look at a motherboard's PCIe diagram, a GPU's spec sheet, and your own budget, and assemble all three into a machine that actually performs the way you expect it to.

# 1. Introduction — The Most Expensive Mistake in Local AI

Earlier in this handbook, we learned that the biggest beginner mistake in local AI is assuming bigger memory automatically means better performance.

There is a second mistake, just as common, and considerably more expensive:

> **"I'll just buy the GPU with the best specs and figure out the rest later."**

The "rest" is not a small detail. The "rest" is the motherboard, the CPU, the power delivery, the cooling, and — above everything else — the physical wiring that connects the GPU to the rest of the computer.

A $2,000 GPU plugged into the wrong slot on the wrong motherboard can perform like a $400 GPU. A pair of GPUs that look identical on a spec sheet can behave completely differently depending on how many lanes of bandwidth the motherboard actually gives each of them.

This is not a software problem. It is not something a driver update will fix. It is physics, baked into the wiring of the board you buy.

So before we get to a single tier list or GPU model number, we need to build the same kind of mental model we built for memory and bandwidth — except this time, the subject is **how hardware actually talks to itself.**

---

# 1.1 Who This Guide Is Written For

Not every reader of this guide has the same budget, and pretending otherwise leads to bad advice. A recommendation that makes sense for someone with $15,000 to spend on a startup's inference lab is useless — or actively harmful — to someone with $2,500 trying to run their first local model.

To keep recommendations honest, this guide is written around three buyer profiles.

## 1.1.1 The Budget Builder ($2,000 – $3,000)

This reader is buying their **first** serious local AI machine, or upgrading from a laptop that can barely run a 7B model.

The goal at this tier is simple: get as much usable VRAM and bandwidth as possible without paying enterprise prices, and accept that some compromises (older GPU generations, used hardware, single-GPU limits) come with the territory.

## 1.1.2 The Indie Builder ($5,000 – $8,000)

This reader already understands the basics and is building something they intend to rely on — a personal multi-model setup, a small side business, or a serious hobby lab.

At this tier, dual-GPU setups become realistic, and the conversation shifts from "what's the single best card" to "how do two or more cards work together."

## 1.1.3 The Startup-Lab Builder ($10,000 – $15,000+)

This reader is building infrastructure for a team, a product, or a serious research workload. Reliability, multi-GPU scaling, and long-term software support start to matter more than raw price-per-token.

At this tier, the conversation includes data-center-class hardware (A100s, RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell cards) and four-or-more-GPU topologies that simply aren't practical on consumer motherboards.

Throughout this chapter, recommendations will be labeled by which of these three buyers they're meant for. If you don't yet know which one you are, a simple rule of thumb works well: **buy for the workload you have today, on hardware that won't actively block you from adding a second GPU later.**

# 1.2 How Hardware Gets Scored in This Guide

"Best GPU" is a meaningless phrase on its own. Best at what? Best for whom? Best compared to what alternative?

To make comparisons fair, every GPU in this guide is evaluated against the same seven criteria. Understanding these criteria matters more than memorizing any single GPU's name, because they're the same questions you should be asking about *any* hardware that isn't covered in this guide.

## 1.2.1 Software Maturity

A GPU is only as useful as the software that can drive it. A card with incredible theoretical performance is worthless if no inference engine has good support for it yet.

NVIDIA's CUDA ecosystem is the most mature today, which is why most "Why" explanations later in this chapter mention specific NVIDIA-first tools (vLLM, TensorRT-LLM). AMD and Tenstorrent are catching up, but a buyer should expect more manual setup, narrower model support, or both.

## 1.2.2 VRAM

This should be a familiar concept by now — it's the memory capacity that determines whether a model fits at all. Revisit the GPU Memory Math chapter if you need a refresher on how to calculate this for a specific model.

## 1.2.3 Performance per Watt and per Dollar ("Perf/W & $")

Two numbers, often confused for one.

**Performance per watt** asks: how much useful work does this card do for every watt of electricity it consumes? This matters because power costs money over time, and because the heat from every watt has to be removed by a fan, a radiator, or an air-conditioned room.

**Performance per dollar** asks: how much useful work does this card do for every dollar it costs to buy? This is the number most beginners look at first — and it's an important one — but it ignores the ongoing electricity and cooling cost that perf/W captures.

A card can win on one and lose on the other. A power-hungry card might still offer the best perf/$ if it's cheap enough to buy used.

## 1.2.4 Multi-GPU Scaling

Some GPUs scale beautifully when you add a second or third card — through fast interconnects like NVLink, through software that's well-optimized for tensor parallelism, or simply through generous PCIe lane allocations. Others scale poorly, either because the software stack isn't ready, or because the physical interconnect (more on this soon) becomes the bottleneck.

## 1.2.5 Reliability — ECC and Thermals

**ECC** stands for **Error-Correcting Code**. It refers to a type of memory that can automatically detect and correct certain memory errors before they corrupt your data. Consumer GPUs (the kind built for gaming) typically lack ECC memory. Workstation and data-center GPUs almost always include it.

For a single chat session, a memory error that goes uncorrected might be invisible. For a long-running production server, hour after hour, day after day, an uncorrected memory error can silently corrupt outputs or crash the process. ECC matters more the longer and more seriously you intend to run the hardware.

**Thermals** refers to how well a card manages its own heat — and, by extension, how often it has to slow itself down ("thermal throttle") to avoid damage. A card that throttles under sustained load will quietly under-deliver on its advertised performance.

## 1.2.6 Availability

A GPU that's perfect on paper but permanently out of stock, or only sold in enterprise quantities through enterprise sales channels, is not a practical recommendation for most readers. Availability — including a healthy used market — is part of the score.

## 1.2.7 Acoustics

Server-grade GPUs are frequently designed to be cooled by the loud, high-static-pressure fans found in a data center rack, not the quieter fans found in a home office or bedroom. A card that sounds like a vacuum cleaner under load might be a poor fit even if every other number looks great. This matters more for the Indie and Budget tiers, where the machine is likely to live somewhere a human has to sit near it.

# 1.3 The Use-Case Lens — What "Best" Actually Means Here

Every recommendation in this guide is filtered through one specific lens: **inference first.**

That means the guide is primarily evaluating GPUs on how well they *run* an already-trained model and generate responses from it — the everyday job of a local AI assistant, chatbot, or coding tool.

Where relevant, notes also call out how a card behaves for:

* **LoRA / QLoRA fine-tuning** — lightweight forms of training that adjust a small number of additional parameters on top of an existing model, rather than retraining the whole thing from scratch. This is far less demanding than full training, and many of the GPUs in this guide can handle it even if they couldn't handle training a model from zero.
* **Hybrid pipelines** — setups that combine a GPU with **CPU offload** (running part of the model on the regular system memory and CPU when it doesn't fully fit in VRAM) and **quantization** (the precision-shrinking techniques from earlier chapters) to stretch a GPU's effective capacity further than its VRAM alone would suggest.

If your priority is full from-scratch model training rather than inference, treat this guide as a starting point, not a final answer — full training has different, usually much higher, hardware demands.

# 1.4 The Software Defaults Behind Every Recommendation

Every "Why" and "Notes" entry later in this chapter assumes a specific piece of software is doing the actual work of running the model. This guide will not go deep into how any of these tools work internally — that's a subject for a later chapter — but you should at least recognize the names, because they explain *why* certain GPUs are recommended over others.

* **vLLM (tensor-parallel)** — A popular, high-throughput inference engine. "Tensor-parallel" refers to a technique for splitting a single model's calculations across multiple GPUs so they work on one request together, which is the default assumption behind most of this guide's multi-GPU recommendations.
* **ExLlama V2 (EXL2)** — A quantization format and runtime built specifically to squeeze strong performance out of consumer NVIDIA GPUs, especially when VRAM is tight.
* **TensorRT-LLM / SGLang** — Two more advanced, highly optimized inference engines that tend to get the most out of NVIDIA's data-center and workstation-class cards.
* **tt-llm** — Tenstorrent's own inference runtime, built specifically for their RISC-V-based AI chips.
* **llama.cpp** — Mentioned here specifically for **CPU offload** scenarios — situations where part of a model has to run on the CPU and system RAM because it doesn't fully fit on the GPU. It is excellent for this and for budget setups, but it is not the assumed default for the higher-tier multi-GPU recommendations in this chapter.

# 1.5 The Topology Rule Hiding Behind Every Multi-GPU Recommendation

Whenever this guide recommends more than one GPU, it is quietly assuming three rules of good multi-GPU hardware design:

```text
Multi-GPU Topology Rules

        │

        ├── Use a Power-of-Two GPU Count (2, 4, 8, 16)

        ├── Give Every GPU the Same Number of Lanes (Uniform x8 Gen4/5)

        └── Keep Cables Short, and Use Certified Redrivers / Retimers
```

**Why a power of two?** Most multi-GPU inference software splits work by repeatedly dividing it in half across GPUs. Two, four, eight, and sixteen GPUs divide evenly. Three, five, or seven GPUs create awkward, uneven splits that are harder for software to balance and that some frameworks don't support cleanly at all.

**Why uniform lanes?** If GPU 1 gets sixteen lanes of bandwidth and GPU 2 only gets four, the entire multi-GPU system will only run as fast as its slowest, most starved card — much like a team of rowers where one person has a smaller oar. Every GPU in a multi-GPU build should get the same PCIe treatment as every other GPU.

**Why short cables and retimers?** PCIe signals degrade with distance, especially at the higher generations. A redriver or retimer is a small chip placed along a PCIe cable or riser that cleans up and re-amplifies the signal so it can travel further without errors — similar to a repeater on a long network cable. We'll define both terms more precisely later in this chapter.

The rest of this chapter exists to explain exactly *why* these three rules matter, starting with the most fundamental concept of all: PCIe itself.

# 1.6 PCIe — The Highway Every GPU Drives On

If you've spent any time reading AI hardware threads, you've almost certainly seen something like "PCIe 4.0 x16" thrown around as though it were self-explanatory. For a beginner, it usually isn't.

So let's start completely from zero.

**PCIe** stands for **Peripheral Component Interconnect Express**. It is the standard, physical interface that your GPU, your SSD, and most other add-on cards use to talk to your CPU. Every modern motherboard is built around a number of these PCIe connections, usually appearing as long slots that cards plug directly into.

A useful way to picture PCIe is as a highway system built into your motherboard:

```text
CPU  ◄────────  PCIe  ────────►  GPU / SSD / Network Card

         (the highway connecting them)
```

Data — model weights, calculations, results — drives back and forth along this highway every time the GPU needs something from the rest of the computer, or needs to send a result back.

Two numbers describe how big and how fast that highway is: the number of **lanes**, and the **generation**.

## 1.6.1 Lanes — How Many Lanes Does the Highway Have?

A **PCIe lane** is a single physical pathway for data, made of a pair of wires: one wire sends data, and one wire receives data, simultaneously. This "send and receive at the same time" property is called **full duplex**, and it's worth remembering — it comes back up later when we calculate real bandwidth numbers.

Lanes are bundled together to form wider connections. You'll commonly see:

* **x1** — one lane (used for things like Wi-Fi cards)
* **x4** — four lanes (commonly used for SSDs)
* **x8** — eight lanes
* **x16** — sixteen lanes (the standard width for a full-size GPU slot)

More lanes means more total bandwidth available at the same time — the same way a six-lane highway can move more cars per minute than a two-lane road, even if every car drives at the same speed.

## 1.6.2 Generations — Doubling the Speed Limit

The "4.0" in "PCIe 4.0" refers to the **generation** of the standard. Each new generation roughly **doubles** the speed of every individual lane, while the number of lanes (x8, x16, etc.) stays a separate, independent choice.

```text
PCIe Generation          Speed per Lane (one direction)

Gen 3                    ≈ 1 GB/s

Gen 4                    ≈ 2 GB/s

Gen 5                    ≈ 4 GB/s

Gen 6                    ≈ 8 GB/s
```

So "PCIe 4.0 x16" tells you two separate things at once: the generation (4.0, meaning roughly 2 GB/s per lane) and the width (x16, meaning sixteen of those lanes working together).

## 1.6.3 Putting Lanes and Generation Together

Multiply the per-lane speed by the number of lanes, and you get the one-directional bandwidth of that connection. Because PCIe is full duplex, the GPU can send and receive at that same rate simultaneously, so the *combined* (both directions added together) bandwidth is double the one-directional number.

```text
PCIe Gen 3 x16:
  16 GB/s one direction
  32 GB/s combined (both directions)

PCIe Gen 4 x16:
  32 GB/s one direction
  64 GB/s combined

PCIe Gen 5 x16:
  64 GB/s one direction
  128 GB/s combined

PCIe Gen 6 x16:
  128 GB/s one direction
  256 GB/s combined
```

A useful sanity check: a full x16 Gen 4 slot moves data roughly four times faster than a typical consumer NVMe SSD. That's how much headroom a full-width, full-generation GPU slot actually has — and it's exactly the headroom that starts disappearing the moment a GPU is forced down to a narrower slot or an older generation.

## 1.6.4 Why This Actually Matters for Inference

During inference — the everyday job of generating a response from a model — most of the heavy lifting happens *inside* the GPU's own VRAM, which is why memory bandwidth (the subject of the previous chapter) matters more than PCIe bandwidth for raw token generation speed.

But PCIe still matters constantly in practice:

* Loading the model from disk into VRAM in the first place.
* Moving data on and off the GPU when a workload doesn't fit entirely in VRAM (CPU offload).
* Multi-GPU setups, where activations and partial results have to travel between GPUs — and on a consumer platform, that traffic often has to cross the PCIe bus, because consumer GPUs don't usually have a faster dedicated link to each other (more on this when we cover NVLink).

A GPU starved of PCIe bandwidth in a multi-GPU build will spend real, measurable time waiting for data instead of computing — exactly the "idle but for a different reason" experience the bandwidth chapter warned about.

## 1.6.5 Why This Matters Even More for Training

If you ever move beyond inference into training or heavy fine-tuning, PCIe bandwidth becomes much more important, for two reasons:

* The **data loader** has to continuously feed fresh training examples from storage into the GPU.
* **Checkpoint writes** — periodically saving the model's current state to disk so progress isn't lost — push large amounts of data back across the same bus.

Starve either of these of bandwidth, and the GPU sits idle waiting for data instead of computing — the exact opposite of what you paid for.

## 1.6.6 The Multi-GPU Lane Squeeze

Here is the detail that catches almost every first-time multi-GPU builder off guard: **a CPU only has so many PCIe lanes to give out, and every slot, NVMe drive, and chipset connection on the motherboard draws from that same pool.**

```text
Total CPU Lanes
        │
        ├── GPU Slot 1
        ├── GPU Slot 2
        ├── NVMe Slot(s)
        └── Chipset Uplink (shared by everything else)
```

On a typical gaming motherboard, plugging in a second GPU is often a trap: the first slot keeps its full x16 connection, but the second slot is frequently wired for only x4 or x8 — because the board simply ran out of lanes to give it. The result is a second GPU that looks identical to the first on a spec sheet, but performs noticeably worse in any workload that depends on PCIe bandwidth.

Workstation platforms like AMD's Threadripper Pro or Intel/AMD server chips solve this by giving the CPU dramatically more lanes to begin with — enough to give *every* GPU slot a full, uninstrangled x16 connection. We'll see exactly what that looks like in the case studies later in this chapter.

## 1.6.7 The Bandwidth Cheat Sheet

A quick reference you'll want to come back to:

```text
RTX 4090 on PCIe Gen 4 x16        → 32 GB/s one direction
Same card dropped to Gen 3 x8     →  8 GB/s one direction

Consumer desktop CPU (16–24 lanes total)   → 1 GPU at x16, or 2 GPUs with one of them bottlenecked
Threadripper (≈72 lanes)                   → 2–4 GPUs at full x16 Gen 4
Threadripper Pro (≈128 lanes)              → 4–6 GPUs at full x16 Gen 5
EPYC Genoa-class server CPU (≈128–160 lanes) → 6–10 GPUs at full x16 Gen 5
```

The pattern should be obvious: **the CPU platform you choose sets a hard ceiling on how many GPUs you can run without starving them**, long before the GPUs themselves become the limiting factor.

# 1.7 PCIe Bifurcation — Splitting One Slot Into Two

So far we've treated a "slot" as a fixed thing — a x16 slot is a x16 slot. In reality, many modern motherboards can take a single physical x16 slot and electrically split it into two independent x8 connections, so that two GPUs can each plug into half of what was originally one slot's worth of lanes.

This trick is called **PCIe bifurcation**.

```text
                CPU
                 │
            (x16 lanes)
                 │
                 ▼
          PCIe Bifurcation
                 │
        ┌────────┴────────┐
        ▼                 ▼
     GPU 0 (x8)       GPU 1 (x8)
```

A few practical things to know about bifurcation:

* It must be **supported and enabled in the motherboard's BIOS**. Not every board supports it, and on the boards that do, it's often an option you have to find and turn on manually — it's rarely the default.
* It typically requires a **bifurcation-capable riser or splitter card** that physically routes the lanes to two separate physical slots or connectors.
* Bifurcating x16 into x8/x8 is not free — you're not gaining lanes, you're dividing the lanes you already had. Each GPU now gets half of what one GPU would have had alone.
* Bifurcating further, like x16 into x4/x4/x4/x4, is possible on some boards but pushes individual GPUs down to a much narrower (and more easily bottlenecked) connection.

Bifurcation is the mechanism that makes practical dual-GPU consumer builds possible at all — it's exactly what's happening in the dual-GPU case study coming up in section 1.9.

# 1.8 CPU-Direct Lanes vs Chipset Lanes — The Hidden Bottleneck

This is, without exaggeration, the single most common multi-GPU mistake this guide will warn you about. It deserves its own section before we look at real motherboard diagrams.

Every motherboard has two fundamentally different kinds of PCIe connections:

```text
CPU
 │
 ├── CPU-Direct PCIe Lanes  →  Full bandwidth, lowest latency
 │
 └── Chipset (PCH)
       │
       └── Chipset-Routed PCIe Lanes  →  Shared, much lower bandwidth, higher latency
```

**CPU-direct lanes** connect straight from the processor to a slot, with nothing in between. This is the fast path, and it's what every GPU slot *should* use.

**Chipset-routed lanes** connect to the motherboard's chipset (also called the PCH, or Platform Controller Hub) first, and the chipset then connects back to the CPU over a single, much narrower **uplink** that the chipset shares between *everything* attached to it — USB ports, SATA drives, onboard audio, secondary M.2 slots, and any PCIe slot wired through the chipset.

## 1.8.1 Why the Uplink Is the Trap

The chipset's uplink to the CPU is dramatically narrower than a direct GPU slot:

* On Intel platforms, this uplink is called **DMI** (Direct Media Interface) — typically around **16 GB/s** total, shared by everything on the chipset.
* On AMD AM5 platforms, the chipset uplink is typically around **8 GB/s** total, also shared by everything on the chipset.

Compare that to a single CPU-direct GPU slot, which can move **30–60+ GB/s** on its own depending on generation and width. A GPU plugged into a chipset-routed slot isn't just slower — it's sharing a connection that's already many times narrower than its own direct alternative, with USB devices, storage, and audio all competing for the same narrow pipe at the same time.

For a single GPU doing light, occasional work, this might never be noticed. For a second GPU expected to participate in multi-GPU inference, it is almost always catastrophic — commonly cutting effective bandwidth down to a few GB/s, and multi-GPU software that depends on fast GPU-to-GPU communication can fail to scale at all.

The rule that follows from this is simple, and it will repeat throughout the rest of this chapter:

> **Every GPU that needs to participate in real multi-GPU work belongs on a CPU-direct PCIe slot. The chipset is for everything else.**

# 1.9 Case Study: A Consumer Dual-GPU Build (AM5 / LGA1700)

Let's make all of this concrete with a real motherboard topology — the kind of layout you'd find on a high-end AM5 (AMD) or LGA1700 (Intel) consumer desktop motherboard being pushed into dual-GPU duty.

```text
                              CPU
              AM5: 24 total lanes  |  LGA1700: 20 total lanes
                                │
              ┌─────────────────┼─────────────────┐
              │                 │                 │
          (x16 PEG)         (x4 NVMe)        (chipset uplink)
              │                 │                 │
              ▼                 ▼                 ▼
       PCIe Bifurcation     CPU NVMe          Chipset (PCH)
         x16 → x8/x8        Direct x4,        Intel DMI 4.0 x8 ≈ 16 GB/s
              │             High Speed        AMD PCIe 4.0 x4 ≈  8 GB/s
       ┌──────┴──────┐                              │
       ▼             ▼                       USB / SATA / Audio
   GPU 0 (x8)    GPU 1 (x8)                  ⚠ Multi-GPU FAILS here
   Primary       Secondary
   CPU-Direct    Shares the same
   No Sharing    x16 bifurcation
```

A few numbers from this layout are worth sitting with:

* The CPU itself only has **24 total lanes on AM5** or **20 total lanes on LGA1700** to distribute across *everything* — both GPU slots, the primary NVMe drive, and the chipset uplink combined. This is a small, tightly rationed budget compared to the workstation platform we'll look at next.
* When the x16 slot is bifurcated into x8/x8, **each** GPU gets roughly **64 GB/s on PCIe Gen 5**, or **32 GB/s on PCIe Gen 4** — half of what a single GPU alone would have received in a full x16 slot.
* The **chipset uplink** — Intel's DMI 4.0 x8 at roughly 16 GB/s, or AMD's chipset link at roughly 8 GB/s — is shared by USB, SATA, and audio in addition to anything else routed through it. This is the slot you must **never** put a second GPU into for serious multi-GPU work; the red "AVOID" path in the diagram above represents a card that's been plugged into a chipset-routed slot instead of a CPU-direct one, and it can drop GPU-to-GPU throughput to roughly **4 GB/s** — a near-total bottleneck for anything multi-GPU.

## 1.9.1 Best Practices for This Platform

* **Verify x8/x8 bifurcation support** in your specific motherboard's manual before assuming it exists — not every board offers it, even within the same chipset family.
* **Use certified Gen 4 or Gen 5 risers only.** Cheap, uncertified risers are a common source of instability at these speeds.
* **Keep both GPUs on CPU-direct slots.** If your board's second slot is chipset-routed rather than bifurcated from the CPU, it is not a suitable home for a second AI-workload GPU.
* **Check your manual for lane conflicts.** Populating certain M.2 NVMe slots can silently steal lanes from a nearby PCIe slot, downgrading it from x16 to x8 or lower without obvious warning.

## 1.9.2 The Performance Summary

```text
GPU 0:    64 GB/s (Gen 5)  /  32 GB/s (Gen 4)
GPU 1:    64 GB/s (Gen 5)  /  32 GB/s (Gen 4)
Total:   128 GB/s (Gen 5)  /  64 GB/s (Gen 4)
Chipset: ~16 GB/s (Intel)  /  ~8 GB/s (AM5)
```

## 1.9.3 Critical Notes

* **M.2 slots can downshift PCIe slots.** Always check the manual carefully — this is one of the most common silent surprises on consumer boards.
* **Some motherboards disable a secondary slot entirely** the moment bifurcation is enabled elsewhere. Read your specific board's lane-sharing table before buying a second GPU expecting it to "just work."

This platform is a perfectly reasonable home for **two** GPUs at the Budget or low Indie tier. It is not a platform you should expect to scale past two without running into hard lane limits.

# 1.10 Case Study: Scaling to Four GPUs on Threadripper Pro (WRX80 / WRX90)

Once a build needs four genuinely fast GPUs, consumer platforms run out of road. This is where workstation-class CPUs — in this example, AMD's **Threadripper Pro** — become necessary rather than optional.

## 1.10.1 The Platform Basics

Threadripper Pro comes in two relevant generations, identified by their socket and chipset:

```text
WRX80 (Socket sWRX8)   — Threadripper Pro 3000/5000 series — PCIe 4.0
WRX90 (Socket sTR5)    — Threadripper Pro 7000 WX series  — PCIe 5.0
```

The headline number that changes everything: a Threadripper Pro CPU offers **up to 128 PCIe lanes**, directly from the processor — roughly five to six times what the AM5/LGA1700 consumer CPUs in the previous case study had to work with.

It also offers **8-channel memory**, rather than the 2-channel memory typical of consumer desktops:

```text
WRX80: DDR4-3200, JEDEC 1 DIMM-per-channel, up to ~2 TB RDIMM/LRDIMM (board-dependent)
WRX90: DDR5-5200, JEDEC 1 DIMM-per-channel, higher OC headroom, up to ~2 TB RDIMM
```

It's worth pausing on one easily-confused point: **memory bandwidth and PCIe lane count are completely independent of each other.** Having 128 PCIe lanes says nothing about how fast your system RAM is, and vice versa. Both matter, but they're separate budgets, and a board's 8 memory channels should be populated symmetrically (the same number and type of DIMM per channel) to get full JEDEC-rated speed.

## 1.10.2 The Full Topology

```text
                          Threadripper Pro CPU
                       (up to 128 CPU-direct lanes)
                                  │
              ┌───────────────────┼────────────────────┐
              │                   │                    │
        CPU Root Complex     64 lanes reserved      Chipset Uplink
        fans lanes out to        for GPUs           WRX80: Gen4 x8 ≈ 15.8 GB/s (each way)
        4–6x PCIe x16 slots          │               WRX90: Gen4 x4 ≈  7.9 GB/s (each way)
        2–4x M.2 NVMe sockets        │               (separate from the 128 device lanes)
        MCIO / SlimSAS connectors    │                       │
        Onboard 10/25GbE NICs        │                       ▼
                                      │              WRX80 / WRX90 Chipset (PCH)
              ┌───────┬───────┬──────┴──┬───────┐    Hangs: USB, SATA, some M.2/SlimSAS,
              ▼       ▼       ▼         ▼        |    audio, IPMI
           GPU 0   GPU 1   GPU 2     GPU 3       |    ⚠ Do NOT attach GPUs or 200G NICs here
          x16_1   x16_2   x16_3     x16_4        |              │
        CPU-Direct, all four slots, full x16     |              ▼
        WRX90: Gen5 (63 GB/s each, uni-dir.)     |     Chipset Peripherals:
        WRX80: Gen4 (31.5 GB/s each, uni-dir.)   |     Bulk/archive NVMe, SATA pools,
                                                  |     USB 3.2/USB4, onboard 1/10GbE,
                                                  |     audio, sensors, low-speed cards
                                                  │
                                          ⚠ Anti-Pattern:
                                       GPU placed on chipset lanes
                                    → 60–80% performance loss typical
```

A separate region of CPU-direct lanes is reserved for **High-Speed I/O** — fast NICs (100G/200G Ethernet, InfiniBand) and primary NVMe storage — kept off the chipset entirely so they share the same low-latency root complex as the GPUs, which directly benefits technologies like GPUDirect RDMA and NCCL that move data straight between a network card and a GPU.

## 1.10.3 The Bandwidth Math

Per-GPU link throughput, assuming a full, uninterrupted x16 CPU-direct connection:

```text
Gen 4 x16:  31.5 GB/s one direction   (≈ 63 GB/s combined, both directions)
Gen 5 x16:  63.0 GB/s one direction   (≈ 126 GB/s combined, both directions)
```

Scaled out to all four GPUs, all running one-directional traffic at the same time:

```text
WRX80 (Gen 4), 4× x16:  4 × 31.5 =  126 GB/s   (≈ 252 GB/s combined)
WRX90 (Gen 5), 4× x16:  4 × 63.0 =  252 GB/s   (≈ 504 GB/s combined)
```

If the motherboard forces any slot down to x8 instead of a full x16 — which happens on some boards once certain M.2 or MCIO connectors are populated — simply halve that GPU's numbers. This is exactly why the golden rule for this platform is to **prioritize keeping all four slots at full x16 CPU-direct**, even if that means choosing different M.2 slots for storage.

## 1.10.4 The Anti-Pattern: A GPU on the Chipset

The diagram includes a deliberate warning path, because this mistake is common enough to deserve repeating in plain language. If a GPU ends up wired through the chipset instead of directly to the CPU, four things go wrong simultaneously:

1. **Uplink bottleneck** — all of that GPU's traffic now has to squeeze through the shared 15.8 GB/s (WRX80) or 7.9 GB/s (WRX90) chipset uplink, instead of its own dedicated lane allocation.
2. **Contention** — that same narrow uplink is also carrying USB, SATA, audio, and anything else hanging off the chipset, all fighting for the same bandwidth at the same time.
3. **Latency** — routing through the chipset adds an extra physical hop, which especially hurts latency-sensitive multi-GPU communication.
4. **Broken peer-to-peer (P2P) communication** — chipset-routed GPUs typically can't participate cleanly in the direct GPU-to-GPU communication patterns (like NCCL/RCCL rings) that multi-GPU inference and training rely on.

The typical real-world result is a **60–80% performance loss** on that GPU. A chipset-routed slot should be reserved for display output or genuinely low-duty peripherals — never for a GPU you expect to do real AI work.

## 1.10.5 Best Practices for 4-GPU Builds

* **Lane allocation strategy:** reserve a full 64 lanes for four GPUs (four x16 slots), and separately allocate 16–32 lanes for high-speed NICs and another 16–32 lanes for primary NVMe storage — out of the 128 lanes available.
* **UEFI/BIOS settings:** enable **Above 4G Decoding** and **Resizable BAR** (settings that allow the system to properly address large amounts of GPU memory — without them, large-VRAM cards can behave strangely or fail to initialize correctly). For PCIe peer-to-peer and NCCL workloads, prefer **ACS Disabled** on the GPU root ports (ACS, Access Control Services, is a PCIe feature that can interfere with direct GPU-to-GPU traffic if left enabled).
* **Physical, power, and cooling:** use a chassis with at least four double-wide slots and straight-through airflow; populate any auxiliary slot-power headers your board provides; plan for 300–600 watts per GPU and multiple 20A circuits at 208–240V for a fully loaded build; monitor PCIe slot temperatures under sustained load; and burn in your risers and retimers ahead of time, since Gen 5 signal integrity is unforgiving of marginal hardware.

## 1.10.6 Critical Notes and Edge Cases

* Board lane maps vary — some WRX90 boards ship with an unusual layout like six x16 slots plus one x8 slot. Always check your specific motherboard's manual.
* Using a CPU-attached M.2 or MCIO connector for extra storage can silently consume x4 or x8 lanes from a nearby slot, sometimes disabling it entirely or dropping it to x8.
* If a slot does drop to x8, prioritize your GPU placement to keep as many GPUs as possible at full x16.
* Beyond four GPUs on this platform, you'll need either dedicated PCIe switch backplanes (which follow different design rules entirely) or a move up to an EPYC server platform.

## 1.10.7 The Golden Rule

```text
Optimal Path:
  All GPUs on CPU-direct PCIe slots → lowest latency, full bandwidth

Secondary Path:
  Remaining CPU-direct lanes → NICs and primary NVMe
  Chipset → bulk I/O only (USB, SATA)

Anti-Pattern:
  Never place a GPU on chipset lanes.
  The shared uplink (15.8 or 7.9 GB/s total) is a hard bottleneck
  no amount of GPU horsepower can work around.
```

128 CPU-direct lanes are enough to give four full-width GPUs *and* high-speed networking *and* fast storage, all without compromise — but only if every connection is mapped deliberately. The motherboard manual's lane table is not optional reading on a platform like this.

# 1.11 Don't Build an AI Server Out of Gaming Parts

Everything in the previous two case studies points toward the same underlying lesson, so let's say it directly: **a build that's perfect for gaming is frequently a poor foundation for AI, even when the GPU itself is identical.**

## 1.11.1 Gaming Motherboards Run Out of Lanes Fast

Gaming motherboards typically offer somewhere between **24 and 48 total PCIe lanes**, shared across every slot, every M.2 drive, and the chipset uplink. As section 1.9 showed, that's barely enough for two GPUs to coexist comfortably, and nowhere near enough for three or more without serious compromises.

## 1.11.2 Gaming CPUs Aren't Built for This Either

Consumer CPUs aimed at gaming (typical Intel Core i9 or AMD Ryzen chips) are designed and validated around single-GPU gaming workloads, not sustained, heavy, multi-GPU AI inference or training. Their lane budgets, memory channel counts, and sustained power delivery all reflect that original design target.

## 1.11.3 RGB RAM Is Not the Same Thing as ECC RDIMMs

It's tempting, when shopping, to treat "RAM is RAM." It isn't. Consumer RAM marketed for gaming PCs (often advertised with RGB lighting) is built for speed and price, not error correction. As section 1.2.5 explained, **ECC** memory can detect and correct certain memory errors automatically — something consumer RAM generally cannot do at all. When you're pushing 100+ GB of data through memory in a long-running AI workload, that error-correction capability stops being a nice-to-have and starts being the difference between a stable server and one that silently corrupts data or crashes unpredictably.

## 1.11.4 "Auto OC" and High Wattage Settings Are Not Your Friend Here

Many consumer motherboards ship with automatic overclocking profiles enabled by default, pushing voltage and clock speeds beyond their officially validated limits in pursuit of extra gaming benchmark scores. Combined with the sustained, heavy load of an AI workload — very different from the bursty nature of gaming — these settings are a common, avoidable cause of instability and random reboots.

## 1.11.5 What to Use Instead

* **Server and workstation motherboards** (ASRock Rack and Supermicro are two well-known examples) typically offer 6–8 full x16 slots, power delivery designed for sustained heavy load, and remote management features built for unattended operation.
* **Workstation and server CPUs** — AMD EPYC, AMD Threadripper Pro, or Intel Xeon W-3400-class chips — offer somewhere in the range of 112–128 PCIe lanes, support ECC memory natively, and are explicitly validated for the kind of sustained, heavy, always-on workloads that AI inference and training actually represent.
* **ECC RDIMMs**, not RGB-branded gaming memory. Stability beats looks every time on a machine meant to run unattended.

The short version: **if you're building anything beyond a single GPU, treat the motherboard and CPU choice as seriously as the GPU choice itself.** A great GPU bottlenecked by a gaming-grade platform is, for all practical purposes, a worse GPU.

# 1.12 The GPU Tier List

With the platform fundamentals out of the way, we can finally talk about specific GPUs.

## 1.12.1 How to Read This List

This tier list ranks GPUs from **S-Tier** (the best available today across the criteria in section 1.2) down through progressively more budget-conscious tiers. Every entry includes a short **Why** — the core reason it earns its tier — and, where relevant, a **Notes** line covering practical gotchas a buyer should know before purchasing.

Remember: this list is filtered through the inference-first lens from section 1.3. A card's tier here reflects how well it serves local AI inference workloads specifically, not gaming performance, not general compute benchmarks.

## 1.12.2 S-Tier — The Best You Can Buy

**NVIDIA H100 80 GB**

* **Why:** An FP8/BF16 powerhouse with mature NVLink and NVSwitch scaling, plus MIG (Multi-Instance GPU) support that lets a single physical card be safely partitioned into several smaller, isolated virtual GPUs for multiple simultaneous tenants or workloads.
* **Notes:** PCIe versions of the H100 anchor 4–8 GPU rigs nicely. The SXM form factor (a different physical packaging used in the densest data-center servers) requires specialized HGX backplane hardware most home or small-lab builders won't have.

**NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell 96 GB**

* **Why:** A single-slot card from NVIDIA's newest "Blackwell" architecture, rated at roughly 4,000 AI TOPS (a marketing measure of raw AI throughput) and approximately **1,792 GB/s** of memory bandwidth — placing it at the very top of the bandwidth landscape covered in the previous chapter.
* **Notes:** Draws up to 600 watts and demands a full PCIe 5.0 x16 connection plus serious airflow or liquid cooling to sustain that power draw reliably.

**NVIDIA A100 80 GB**

* **Why:** The reigning stability benchmark of the data-center world — an extremely mature software stack and full MIG support.
* **Notes:** Plenty of used units are available on the secondary market at reasonable prices, but inspect a unit's ECC error counters before buying used — a history of frequent corrected errors can be an early warning sign of a degrading card.

**Tenstorrent Blackhole p150a 32 GB**

* **Why:** A RISC-V-based AI System-on-Chip (SoC) — meaning the processing architecture itself is built on the open RISC-V instruction set rather than a traditional GPU design — delivering greater than 2.5 TFLOPS of compute per watt, an exceptional efficiency figure.
* **Notes:** Uses a QSFP-800 high-speed networking connector to mesh multiple cards together (more on this in the budget sections below). Software support is real but still earlier-stage than NVIDIA's, and kernel-level porting work is ongoing.

## 1.12.3 A-Tier — Excellent, With Trade-offs

**NVIDIA GeForce RTX 5090 32 GB**

* **Why:** The best consumer performance-per-dollar available today, paired with 32 GB of fast GDDR7 memory.
* **Notes:** Has no NVLink connector, meaning multi-card setups must rely on PCIe alone for GPU-to-GPU communication. Watch for cut-down "D" or "DD" variants of this card — check the specific model's memory bandwidth before assuming every RTX 5090 is identical.

**NVIDIA L40S 48 GB**

* **Why:** An Ada-generation data-center card with FP8 support plus the graphics and media encode/decode blocks a pure compute card (like the A100) lacks — useful if your pipeline also needs video processing alongside inference.
* **Notes:** Plays nicely in 4–8 GPU PCIe-based rigs without needing NVLink infrastructure.

**NVIDIA RTX 6000 Ada 48 GB**

* **Why:** The ECC-equipped workstation champion for dual- and quad-GPU builds, combining data-center-grade reliability with a more workstation-friendly form factor.
* **Notes:** Mind the card's physical length in smaller cases, and plan your power cabling around its **12VHPWR** connector — a newer, compact power connector standard capable of delivering more power through fewer pins than older connectors, but one that requires careful seating and routing to avoid connection issues.

**NVIDIA A40 48 GB**

* **Why:** A quieter Ampere-generation card with full ECC support, and plentiful availability on the used market.

**NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 24 GB**

* **Why:** Backed by the largest community of any card on this list, with stellar performance-per-dollar for single-GPU ("solo") builders specifically.

# 1.13 Quick Picks by Budget

If the tier list above feels like more detail than you need right now, this section distills it down to direct, buy-this recommendations for each of the three buyer profiles from section 1.1.

## 1.13.1 Budget Tier ($2,000 – $3,000)

* **NVIDIA:** GeForce RTX 4090 or RTX 3090, run through **llama.cpp** or **EXL2** with the card's power limit reduced (lowering the maximum wattage a card is allowed to draw — this trims a small amount of performance in exchange for noticeably lower heat and power draw, often a good trade for a card running in a home office).

# 1.14 Scaling the Build — From 1 GPU to 16 GPUs

Putting the case studies, the tier list, and the budget picks together, here's how a build's *design priorities* — not just its part list — change as it grows.

## 1.14.1 One GPU — The Baseline

A single GPU on almost any reasonably modern consumer motherboard gets a full x16 slot with no sharing and no bifurcation to worry about. This is the simplest, most forgiving build to get right, and it's exactly where the Budget tier from section 1.13.1 lives. The only real decisions are which card fits your VRAM and bandwidth needs, and whether your power supply and case airflow can support it.

## 1.14.2 Two GPUs — Bifurcation Territory

This is the consumer dual-GPU case study from section 1.9. The defining decision becomes whether your motherboard can split its x16 slot into a uniform x8/x8 — and whether anything else on the board (an M.2 drive, a third slot) is quietly stealing lanes from that split. This tier is realistic on AM5 or LGA1700 platforms, but it is also their practical ceiling.

## 1.14.3 Four GPUs — Workstation Territory

Four genuinely fast GPUs, each on a full, uncompromised x16 CPU-direct slot, generally requires moving up to a workstation platform like Threadripper Pro (section 1.10). This is also roughly where the Indie and Startup-Lab tiers begin to diverge from the Budget tier in build complexity: power delivery, cooling, and chassis design all start to require deliberate planning rather than default assumptions.

## 1.14.4 Eight GPUs — Server Territory

Beyond four GPUs, even Threadripper Pro's 128 lanes start to feel tight once high-speed networking and storage are accounted for alongside the GPUs themselves. This is the territory of EPYC-class server CPUs, with their larger lane budgets, and increasingly of dedicated **PCIe switch backplanes** — specialized hardware that sits between the CPU and a large bank of GPU slots, managing how a smaller pool of CPU lanes is shared and switched among more physical slots than the CPU could directly support on its own. NVLink (where the GPUs support it) starts to matter far more at this scale, since it offloads GPU-to-GPU traffic from the PCIe bus entirely rather than competing for the same lanes used to reach the CPU.

## 1.14.5 Sixteen GPUs — Cluster Territory

At sixteen GPUs, a single motherboard's lane budget — no matter how generous — typically isn't the whole story anymore. Builds at this scale commonly span **multiple physical servers**, connected to each other over fast networking (often InfiniBand or high-speed Ethernet) rather than relying on PCIe to reach every card directly. This reintroduces concepts from the previous chapter's discussion of multi-GPU scaling: topology (how servers and GPUs are physically wired to each other), RDMA (letting servers access each other's memory directly with minimal overhead), and synchronization overhead all become first-order design concerns, not afterthoughts. This is squarely enterprise and research-lab territory, well past what this guide's three buyer profiles are built around — but it's worth knowing the shape of what lies beyond a single workstation, if your ambitions ever grow that far.

```text
1 GPU   →  Simple, full x16, no sharing
2 GPUs  →  Bifurcation (x8/x8), CPU-direct discipline matters
4 GPUs  →  Workstation CPU required for uncompromised x16 × 4
8 GPUs  →  Server CPU + PCIe switches, NVLink starts to matter
16 GPUs →  Multi-server clusters, networking becomes the new "lane budget"
```

# 1.15 Tools for Watching Your Hardware Breathe

Once a build is running, you'll want to actually *see* what's happening inside it — not just trust that it's working. `nvidia-smi`, the default NVIDIA monitoring command, is useful but limited. A few additional Linux tools fill the gaps:

* **btop** — A clean, modern, full-screen system monitor covering CPU and GPU stats, with a wide range of visual themes. A good general-purpose "what's my whole machine doing right now" view.
* **glances** — An all-in-one overview combining CPU, memory, disk, and network statistics in a single, dense screen — useful for a quick health check across every subsystem at once.
* **nvtop / nvitop** — Purpose-built GPU monitors that go well beyond `nvidia-smi`'s basic numbers, showing live graphs of GPU utilization, PCIe traffic, power draw, and temperature. This is the most direct way to actually *see* a PCIe bottleneck happening in real time, rather than inferring one from slow token generation.
* **duf** — A modern, more readable replacement for the traditional `df` disk usage command, useful for keeping an eye on storage space as model files and checkpoints accumulate.

None of these tools are required to build or run an AI machine, but all four make it far easier to confirm that your carefully-planned PCIe topology is actually behaving the way the manual — and this chapter — promised it would.

# 1.16 A Few Terms You'll Keep Running Into

A handful of terms tend to show up constantly on spec sheets and in build threads without ever being explained. Quick definitions, so a future spec sheet doesn't stop you cold:

* **12VHPWR** — A compact, high-current power connector standard used on many recent high-power GPUs, capable of delivering more power through fewer pins than older connectors. Requires careful, fully-seated cabling to avoid connection problems at sustained high power draw.
* **MCIO / SlimSAS** — Modern, compact cable-and-connector standards used to route high-speed PCIe signals (for NVMe storage, or sometimes GPUs) away from the motherboard over a cable, rather than requiring a card to plug directly into a slot. Common on workstation and server boards with dense lane counts to distribute.
* **Redriver** — A simple chip placed along a PCIe signal path that re-amplifies the signal without altering its timing, helping it survive longer cable runs or noisier connections.
* **Retimer** — A more advanced chip that does what a redriver does, but also actively cleans up and resynchronizes the signal's timing — necessary at higher PCIe generations (Gen 5 and beyond), where signal integrity margins shrink considerably.
* **ECC VRAM** — Error-Correcting Code memory on the GPU itself (see section 1.2.5), as opposed to ECC on the system's main memory.
* **Lane Budget** — An informal but useful way of describing the total number of PCIe lanes a CPU has available to distribute across every slot, NVMe drive, and chipset connection on a motherboard — the central constraint behind nearly everything in sections 1.6 through 1.10.

# 1.17 The Buying Checklist

After all of the case studies, tier lists, and budget breakdowns, the actual buying decision can be reduced to a short, ordered checklist.

```text
1.

How Many GPUs Does My Workload Actually Need?



2.

Does My Budget Tier Match That GPU Count?



3.

Does My Chosen Platform Give Every GPU a Full,
CPU-Direct Lane Allocation — Not a Chipset-Routed One?



4.

Is My Memory ECC, and Is My Power/Cooling Sized
for Sustained Load, Not Just a Burst?



5.

Have I Verified the Specific Tier-List GPU's "Notes"
Against My Actual Use Case?
```

Skipping any one of these questions is exactly how a buyer ends up with a GPU that performs far below its spec sheet — not because the GPU was a bad choice, but because the platform around it was never asked to support it properly.

# 1.18 The Real Question

Early in this chapter, the question on the table was:

> **"What GPU should I buy?"**

By now, that question should feel incomplete on its own — the same way "what hardware should I buy" felt incomplete by the end of the previous chapter.

The more useful question, the one experienced builders actually ask themselves, is:

> **"What platform am I building around this GPU — and does that platform actually let it breathe?"**

```text
GPU Choice
        │
        ▼
Platform Choice
        │
        ├── Lane Budget
        ├── CPU-Direct vs Chipset
        ├── Topology (Bifurcation / Workstation / Server)
        ├── Memory Reliability (ECC)
        └── Power and Cooling
```

A great GPU on the wrong platform is, in every practical sense, a worse GPU. A modest GPU on a platform built to actually feed it can outperform expectations.

That is the real lesson of this chapter — and it's the lesson worth carrying into every future hardware decision this handbook covers.
